Christopher Columbus was born around 1435 in Genoa to humble circumstances. His father was a wool carder who ensured his children received education. Young Christopher studied reading, writing, mathematics, Latin, and drawing, later attending the University of Pavia where he focused on geometry, geography, astronomy, and navigation. At age 14, Columbus began his seafaring career under his relative Colombo, an admiral commanding Genoese forces. The seas were dangerous, controlled by pirates, requiring all merchant vessels to be heavily armed.

Columbus eventually settled in Lisbon, Portugal's prominent port, where he studied extensively, reading Aristotle, Seneca, and Marco Polo's accounts. Cardinal Aliaco's Cosmographie particularly captivated him, fueling his conviction that unknown lands lay westward across the Atlantic. He proposed his plan to Portuguese King John II, requesting to be named viceroy of discovered territories and claiming one-tenth of profits. The king found his demands excessive for an unknown sea captain. Rejected repeatedly by Genoa's government and various Spanish nobles, Columbus persisted through hardship and disappointment.

At a monastery near Palos, the prior became convinced of Columbus's merit and wrote Queen Isabella. This pivotal letter changed everything. Isabella ultimately embraced his vision, even pledging her personal jewels as collateral for financing. Columbus spent years negotiating terms, demanding titles of Admiral and Viceroy, plus a percentage of all wealth found. Many advisors counseled against it, calling his geography faulty. Yet Isabella believed.

On August 3, 1492, Columbus departed from Palos with three small vessels, the Santa Maria, Pinta, and Nina, carrying approximately 120 men. The crew, largely pressed into service, grew terrified crossing the unknown ocean. Days stretched into weeks with nothing but open water. Sailors whispered of mutiny. Columbus kept two logs, one showing shorter distances to calm fears.

After weeks at sea, signs of land appeared: floating seaweed, migrating birds, and driftwood. On October 12, 1492, at two in the morning, a lookout on the Pinta spotted land. Columbus named the island San Salvador, believing he had reached the outer territories of the Indies. He planted the Spanish flag and claimed the land for Ferdinand and Isabella.

Columbus encountered indigenous peoples he called Indians, finding them gentle, generous, and unclothed. He searched unsuccessfully for gold, instead discovering new plants, animals, and landscapes of remarkable beauty. He visited multiple islands including what is now Cuba, which he initially believed was mainland Asia. He documented the extraordinary natural wealth and beauty of the Caribbean.

On Christmas Day 1492, the Santa Maria ran aground on a reef off Hispaniola. Using timber from the wrecked ship, Columbus built a small fort called La Navidad, leaving 39 men behind as he returned to Spain with news of his discovery. The return voyage was perilous, with terrible storms nearly sinking the Nina.

Columbus arrived back in Spain in March 1493 to a hero's welcome. He paraded through the streets with captured natives, gold trinkets, and exotic parrots. Ferdinand and Isabella received him at court with great honor, confirming his titles of Admiral and Viceroy.

Columbus made three more voyages. His second voyage in 1493 brought 17 ships and 1,200 men to establish colonies. He found La Navidad destroyed and all 39 men dead. He founded Isabella, the first European town in the Americas. But governance was not his strength. The colonies suffered from disease, starvation, and brutal treatment of the native population.

His third voyage in 1498 brought him to the South American mainland for the first time, reaching what is now Venezuela. But complaints about his administration had reached Spain. In 1500, a royal commissioner arrested Columbus and sent him back to Spain in chains. Though Ferdinand and Isabella freed him, they stripped him of his governorship.

His fourth and final voyage in 1502 explored Central America, seeking a passage to Asia that did not exist. Stranded in Jamaica for a year with rotting ships, Columbus used his knowledge of an upcoming lunar eclipse to convince the natives to provide food.

Columbus died on May 20, 1506, in Valladolid, Spain, still believing he had reached Asia. He never understood the true magnitude of his discovery: that he had opened European contact with two vast continents previously unknown to the Old World. His voyages fundamentally changed the course of human history, for better and worse, connecting hemispheres that had been separated for thousands of years.
