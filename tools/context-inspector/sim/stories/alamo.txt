The siege and final assault on the Alamo in 1836 stands as the most celebrated military engagement in Texas history. The battle drew together numerous illustrious personalities, including Tennessee congressman David Crockett, entrepreneur James Bowie, and Mexican president Antonio Lopez de Santa Anna. William Barret Travis, though not nationally famous initially, earned lasting recognition as the Alamo's commander. For many Americans and Texans, the battle symbolizes patriotic sacrifice, though popular depictions often obscure historical reality.

To comprehend the actual battle, one must understand its strategic context within the Texas Revolution. In December 1835, a Federalist army of Texian immigrants, American volunteers, and Tejano allies captured the town from Centralist forces during the siege of Bexar. This victory caused most Texan volunteers to return home. However, provisional government officials feared a spring Centralist offensive. Two main roads threatened Texas from Mexico's interior: the Atascosito Road through San Patricio and Goliad, and the Old San Antonio Road through San Antonio de Bexar. Two forts guarded these approaches, Presidio La Bahia at Goliad and the Alamo at San Antonio, functioning as frontier sentries.

James Clinton Neill commanded the Bexar garrison, while James Walker Fannin Jr. led at Goliad. Most settlers had returned home, so newly arrived American volunteers comprised the majority at both posts. Neill and Fannin resolved to delay the Centralists on the frontier, yet harbored no illusions, without reinforcements, neither position could withstand prolonged siege.

Bexar housed approximately twenty-one artillery pieces of various calibers. Neill, experienced in artillery and holding a regular army commission, logically assumed command. Throughout January, he fortified the mission. Chief engineer Major Green B. Jameson installed most cannons on walls, boasting to General Sam Houston that defenders could whip 10 to 1 with their artillery. Such optimism proved unfounded. The distant garrison suffered severe provisioning shortages. On January 14, Neill informed Houston his people occupied a torpid, defenseless condition, warning that without reinforcements and supplies, they would become an easy prey if attacked.

By January 17, Houston questioned maintaining the Bexar garrison's wisdom. He informed Governor Henry Smith that Colonel James Bowie and volunteers had departed for San Antonio, noting he had ordered the fortifications in the town of Bexar to be demolished and requesting authorization to blow up the Alamo and abandon the place. However, Houston explicitly requested Smith's consent, not unilaterally ordering abandonment. Smith ultimately refused to authorize Houston's proposal.

On January 19, Bowie arrived at the Alamo and found the mission impressively fortified. Neill, recognizing that leaving the camino real unguarded endangered all Anglo settlements, convinced Bowie of the Alamo's strategic importance. Neill's arguments electrified Bowie, who wrote Smith he could not eulogize the conduct and character of Colonel Neill too highly. On February 2, Bowie assured Smith that he and Neill had resolved to die in these ditches rather than surrender.

Neill had lamented lacking horses to send reconnaissance patrols. Now committed to strengthening Bexar's garrison, Smith directed Lieutenant Colonel William B. Travis to bring his Legion of Cavalry. Only thirty horsemen answered the summons. Travis protested to Governor Smith, refusing to risk his reputation going off into the enemy's country with such little means. He threatened resignation, but Smith ignored his objections. Travis reluctantly obeyed, traveling to Bexar with thirty troopers. On February 3, Travis reached the Alamo, soon becoming committed to Neill and describing the fort as Texas's key. About February 8, David Crockett arrived with American volunteers.

On February 14, Neill departed on furlough due to family illness. Travis became acting post commander. The garrison split along party lines: regulars supported Travis; volunteers supported Bowie. Ultimately, the commanders reached compromise, Bowie commanding volunteers, Travis the regulars, both co-signing all orders.

Santa Anna's Centralist army reached the Rio Grande. Though Travis doubted Santa Anna could arrive before March 15, the general appeared on February 23. Travis immediately dispatched a hastily written message to Gonzales: The enemy in large force is in sight. We want men and provisions. The garrison's fate now depended on support from the General Council, Fannin at Goliad, and other Texan volunteers.

Santa Anna demanded surrender. Travis answered with a cannonball. Centralist artillery began battering the walls. On February 24, Travis assumed full command when Bowie fell to mysterious illness. Travis wrote his famous letter to the people of Texas and all Americans in the world, pledging he would never surrender or retreat, swearing Victory or Death. On March 1, thirty-two troops from Gonzales penetrated the enemy cordon and entered the Alamo. On March 3, Travis expressed lost faith in Fannin: I look to the colonies alone for aid.

On March 5, Santa Anna announced an assault for the following day. Around 5:00 AM on Sunday, March 6, he launched columns from four directions. Texan gunners unleashed canister into approximately 1,800 advancing assault troops. Mexican soldiers halted, reformed, and drove forward, eventually breaching defensive perimeters. Travis fell among the first defenders on the north bastion. Combatants withdrew to the Long Barracks, where savage hand-to-hand fighting erupted. Bowie, too ravaged by illness to rise from his bed, received no mercy. The chapel fell last. By dawn, Centralists controlled the compound. The assault lasted ninety minutes. As many as seven defenders survived, but Santa Anna ordered their execution. By eight o'clock, every Alamo fighter lay dead. Currently, 189 defenders appear officially listed, though ongoing research may increase the tally to approximately 257.

Though Santa Anna claimed victory, common soldiers paid the anticipated price. Mexican casualties numbered approximately 600 killed and wounded. Mexican officers led noncombatant women, children, and slaves from the compound. Santa Anna treated female and child captives with admirable courtesy. Famous survivors included Susanna Dickinson, widow of Captain Almeron Dickinson, and their infant daughter Angelina. Mrs. Dickinson traveled to Gonzales, reporting the fort's fall to General Houston, precipitating the dramatic exodus called the Runaway Scrape.

The Alamo's defenders were valiant soldiers. Governor Smith and the General Council ordered Neill, Bowie, and Travis to hold the fort until support arrived. Despite heroic rhetoric, they were not suicidal. Throughout the thirteen-day siege, Travis continuously appealed for promised governmental support. Defenders willingly risked death protecting their country, but death represented a danger accepted, never their objective. The ineffectual provisional government failed delivering promised relief, and Travis's command paid that political dereliction's cost. Even stripped of chauvinistic exaggeration, the Alamo battle remains inspiring in Texas history. Travis and his command's sacrifice animated Texans and kindled righteous fury that defeated Mexicans at San Jacinto. Since 1836, American soldiers worldwide have answered the call: Remember the Alamo!
